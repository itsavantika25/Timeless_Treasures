Structures that still speak

Delhi | 1190s

Qutub Minar

Indo-Islamic architecture marked by red sandstone, inscriptions, and layered dynastic history. It stands 73 meters tall, built by Qutb-ud-din Aibak in 1193. This UNESCO World Heritage Site features five distinct storeys with intricate carvings and verses from the Quran.

Status
Protected monument
Hidden detail
Arabic and Nagari inscriptions trace multiple building phases.

Rajasthan | 1799

Hawa Mahal

A palace facade designed for ventilation, privacy, and urban spectacle in Jaipur's royal streetscape. It was constructed in 1799 by Maharaja Sawai Pratap Singh. Its distinct pink sandstone façade contains 953 small windows (jharokhas) designed for royal women to observe street festivals while maintaining purdah. The five-storey structure resembles Lord Krishna's crown and exemplifies Rajputana architecture with its honeycomb design.

Status
Conserved heritage landmark
Style
Rajput and Mughal influences

Odisha | 13th century

Konark Sun Temple

A monumental stone chariot dedicated to Surya, celebrated for scale, sculpture, and astronomical symbolism. Built in the 13th century by King Narasimhadeva I, it resembles a colossal chariot with twelve pairs of intricately carved stone wheels. Dedicated to Surya, the Sun God, this UNESCO site showcases Kalinga architecture through intricate erotic sculptures and astronomical precision.

Status
UNESCO World Heritage Site
Threat
Weathering and structural fragility

Tamil Nadu | 1010

Brihadeeswarar Temple

A Chola masterpiece that combines devotion, geometry, mural traditions, and engineering ambition. Thanjavur's Brihadeshwara Temple, completed in 1010 CE by Raja Raja Chola I, this temple is the epitomy of Dravidian architecture. Its 66-meter vimana (tower) is crowned by an 80-ton granite capstone.

Status
UNESCO World Heritage Site
Style
Dravidian architecture
